# Measuring Psychological Safety to Unlock Organisational Performance

## Client Context

A large, complex organisation operating in a high-stakes technical environment conducted an annual staff survey to assess organisational health and culture.

While the survey captured broad engagement indicators, leadership wanted to understand a deeper question:

> Do our people feel psychologically safe to speak up, admit mistakes, challenge decisions, and ask for help?

Psychological safety is widely recognised as a foundational driver of learning, innovation, operational excellence, and risk mitigation, particularly in complex and technical organisations.

Deep Field was engaged to evaluate the existing survey instrument, assess its alignment with validated psychological safety frameworks, analyse the results in depth, and provide evidence-based recommendations for cultural intervention.

---

## The Engineering Challenge

Most organisations run engagement surveys. Few measure culture with scientific precision.

Psychological safety is not the same as:

- Policy clarity
- Code of conduct compliance
- Organisational trust
- Employee satisfaction

It is specifically defined as:

> A shared belief that one will not be punished or humiliated for speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes.

The challenge was twofold:

1. Determine whether the existing survey actually measured psychological safety in a valid way.
2. Extract meaningful insight from the data to inform practical organisational change.

---

## Deep Field Approach

We treated the assignment as a systems diagnosis exercise, not a surface-level survey review.

### 1) Framework Benchmarking

We benchmarked the existing survey questions against Amy Edmondson's validated 7-item Psychological Safety Scale, the global academic benchmark in this field.

This allowed us to:

- Identify alignment between existing questions and validated constructs
- Highlight missing dimensions
- Evaluate construct validity
- Assess risk of partial or misleading interpretation

The benchmarking revealed:

- One strong alignment with validated psychological safety constructs
- Two partial overlaps
- Three critical missing dimensions
- Two questions measuring organisational trust and policy clarity rather than interpersonal safety

This distinction is essential. Without full construct coverage, conclusions about psychological safety may be incomplete or inaccurate.

### 2) Quantitative Insight Analysis

We conducted a detailed evaluation of survey results, including:

- Response distribution analysis
- Gap identification between perceived policy strength and lived experience
- Signal extraction from aggregate percentages
- Identification of likely team-level variation masked by organisational averages

Key findings included:

- Moderate confidence in the ability to express dissenting views
- Moderate trust that issues raised would be handled appropriately
- Strong agreement that harassment and discrimination policies exist

The critical insight:

Policy confidence was significantly higher than practical confidence in speaking up.

This indicates structural clarity without full interpersonal safety.

### 3) Cultural Risk Identification

Our analysis identified three systemic risks.

#### Incomplete Measurement Risk

Without measuring all validated psychological safety dimensions, including fear of punishment for mistakes, ease of asking for help, and fear of interpersonal rejection, leadership lacks a full diagnostic picture.

#### Aggregation Risk

Organisation-wide averages conceal team-level variation. Psychological safety is highly localised and can vary dramatically between departments.

#### Intervention Mismatch Risk

If psychological safety is incorrectly diagnosed, interventions may focus on policy communication rather than behavioural leadership practices.

---

## Strategic Recommendations

Deep Field provided a staged, practical roadmap.

### Short-Term Actions

1. Measure correctly by incorporating all seven validated psychological safety items into future surveys.
2. Educate the organisation with a shared understanding of psychological safety and its link to performance, learning, and risk reduction.
3. Go local by analysing and discussing results at the team level to identify pockets of strength and concern.

### Medium-Term Actions

4. Align leadership behavior by training leaders in safe dissent, constructive failure discussions, and open dialogue.
5. Facilitate structured team dialogues using survey results as a catalyst for improvement.
6. Integrate a feedback loop to measure progress and track cultural shifts over time.

## System-Level Impact

This engagement demonstrated a critical organisational truth:

Culture cannot be improved without first being measured accurately.

A poorly constructed survey creates the illusion of insight. A well-constructed one creates clarity.

By aligning measurement with validated research and combining it with rigorous data interpretation, organisations gain:

- Clear visibility into interpersonal risk climates
- Actionable cultural diagnostics
- Reduced blind spots in leadership assumptions
- Targeted intervention design
