# Digitising Spare Manufacturing for High-Voltage Infrastructure

## Client Context

A national power utility operating across high-voltage distribution networks faced a structural challenge common in both developing and mature economies:

- Long lead times for non-critical spares
- High inventory carrying costs
- OEM permutation complexity
- Limited prototyping flexibility
- Operational inefficiencies in live substations

At the same time, additive manufacturing (3D printing) was becoming more accessible, but its applicability in high-voltage environments was largely unverified.

The central question was: can additive manufacturing be engineered and validated for safe use in high-voltage infrastructure environments?

---

## The Engineering Challenge

High-voltage systems are unforgiving. Materials must demonstrate:

- Sufficient dielectric strength
- Predictable breakdown behavior
- Controlled mechanical properties
- Stability under electrical stress

Commercially available 3D printing filaments had no reliable dielectric performance data suitable for substation deployment.

Before operational use could be considered, the material needed to be qualified against recognized standards.

---

## Deep Field Approach

We treated the challenge not as a printing problem, but as a systems validation problem.

### 1) Controlled Additive Manufacturing Framework

We established a controlled fabrication process using:

- FDM (Fused Deposition Modelling) methodology
- Defined layer thickness (0.09 mm)
- Maximum infill
- Tight dimensional tolerances (+/-0.05 mm)
- Repeatable specimen geometry (60 mm x 60 mm x 1 mm)

The objective was not to "print parts" but to ensure process repeatability.

Manufacturing became software-driven and parameter-controlled.

### 2) IEC-Aligned Dielectric Testing Programme

To determine electrical suitability, we designed and executed a breakdown voltage test programme aligned to:

- IEC 60243-1
- ASTM D149 methodology

Testing included:

- Rapid rise breakdown tests
- 20-second step tests
- 60-second step tests
- Slow rate of rise tests
- Very slow rate of rise tests

Each test was repeated five times per condition to establish statistical reliability.

Result:

- Mean dielectric breakdown strength: approximately 24 kV/mm

For context:

- Oil-impregnated transformer board, a commonly used insulating material, measured approximately 25 kV/mm under similar rapid-rise conditions

This demonstrated that properly manufactured FDM components could exhibit dielectric performance within the range of traditional insulating materials.

---

## Controlled Deployment Strategy

Despite promising electrical characteristics, we implemented a staged risk approach.

Initial deployment was limited to:

- Non-direct high-voltage contact applications
- Non-critical spare components

This allowed operational integration without compromising system safety.

---

## Operational Implementation

### Case 1: Live Substation Inspection Tool

Substation operators required improved visibility of primary plant equipment during live inspections. Traditional approaches required specialized live-work teams and equipment.

We engineered and rapidly prototyped a 3D printed attachment for a link-stick (hot-stick), enabling operators to:

- Mount a camera
- Capture elevated inspection footage
- Operate under live conditions
- Reduce reliance on specialist live-work crews

Outcome:

- Reduced inspection overhead
- Lower operational cost
- Increased situational awareness
- Minimal training required

### Case 2: Digital Non-Critical Spare Ecosystem

Utilities often maintain large inventories of non-critical spares due to OEM variation. One example is Bucholtz valve handles, which vary significantly across manufacturers.

Instead of stocking all permutations physically, we:

- Digitally modelled the variants
- Established printable design libraries
- Produced components on demand

Impact:

- Reduced physical inventory requirements
- Lower storage footprint
- Faster response to maintenance events
- Improved spare optimization

This marked a shift from physical inventory systems to digital spare ecosystems.

## System-Level Impact

This initiative delivered more than cost savings. It demonstrated that:

- Additive manufacturing can be validated for infrastructure environments
- Digital design libraries can replace physical spare storage
- Rapid prototyping accelerates operational innovation
- Manufacturing constraints can be removed from early-stage design thinking

The most significant outcome was cultural.

By introducing controlled additive capability inside the engineering department, innovation cycles accelerated. Engineers could test ideas previously dismissed due to tooling cost or manufacturing constraints.

Additive manufacturing became an enabler of engineered experimentation.
